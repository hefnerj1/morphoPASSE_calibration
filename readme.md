# Assessing Calibration of Posterior Probabilities in Morphoscopic Sex Estimation


> Code used for the manuscript

# ABSTRACT
_Morphoscopic traits are widely used in forensic anthropology for estimating biological sex, particularly when metric approaches are limited or skeletal preservation favors qualitative assessment. Recent methodological developments have emphasized probabilistic classification frameworks that report posterior probabilities and other measures of confidence for a sex estimation. Programs that provide predicted sex classifications are most often accompanied by posterior probabilities that are interpreted, explicitly or implicitly, as an indicator of the strength of a classification. However, few studies have empirically evaluated whether these posterior probabilities are properly calibrated and if the results produced by these programs provide enough nuance for complete interpretation. In this study, we evaluate the calibration of posterior probabilities produced by MorphoPASSE, a computer program for the estimation of biological sex from cranial and pelvic traits. To assess how MorphoPASSE handles data, we use a paired analytical strategy combining simulated datasets and known-sex reference samples. Simulated cranial and pelvic datasets were constructed to preserve realistic trait structure while excluding sex-structured signals. In this way, we can empirically quantify the estimation of trait-signal ceilings for these data. Known-sex data from the University of Tennessee Donated Skeletal Collection were analyzed in parallel with the simulated data. Posterior probabilities generated by MorphoPASSE were compared against empirically derived signal ceilings and also observed classification accuracy generated by the program. Across both simulated and known-sex datasets, MorphoPASSE posterior probabilities consistently exceeded trait-signal ceilings and empirical accuracy. This pattern was robust across skeletal regions and persisted under idealized analytical conditions. Calibration analyses further demonstrated systematic overconfidence, with posterior probabilities overstating observed performance, particularly for pelvic traits. These results indicate that posterior probabilities produced by MorphoPASSE are not calibrated to the signal present in the morphoscopic traits used in the program. We conclude that MorphoPASSE posterior probabilities should not be interpreted as direct probabilities of sex membership. Instead, greater emphasis should be placed on reporting classificatory outcomes alongside empirical performance metrics and explicit acknowledgment of uncertainty to support defensible inference in forensic anthropological practice._
